Data Cleaning

Datasets:
In this project, we have used two main types of datasets: Air Quality Index (AQI) and Income.

The links to access these datasets are:

  • AQI: https://aqs.epa.gov/aqsweb/airdata/download_files.html#Daily
  • Income: https://www.census.gov/data/tables/2024/demo/income-poverty/p60-282.html

Datasets Description:
The AQI datasets from the United States Environment Protection Agency (EPA) provide a wide variety of records. In this study, we chose to use datasets for four main pollutants: Ozone (O), Sulfur Dioxide (SO), Carbon Monoxide (CO), and Nitrogen Dioxide (NO), along with four key meteorological variables: Wind Speed/Direction, Temperature, Barometric Pressure, and Relative Humidity/Dewpoint.

These pollutants are common in urban areas and have known health impacts. Meteorological variables were included for their role in shaping how pollutants spread and accumulate over time and space.

Merging AQI and Socioeconomic Data

We loaded AQI data (FINAL_24StatesAQI.csv) and 2023 socioeconomic indicators (ACSDP1Y2023.DP03-Data.csv) including median income, unemployment rate, and poverty rate.

Key steps:

  • Standardized State and County names for consistency.
  • Extracted and cleaned socioeconomic data, removing suffixes like "county", "parish", and "city".
  • Merged both datasets on standardized County Name and State Name.
  • Converted relevant columns to numeric and dropped rows with missing values.
  • Classified counties into 'Good for Living' and 'Bad for Living' based on thresholds for AQI (100), income ($60,000), unemployment (7%), and poverty (15%).

The final merged dataset was saved as Merged_AQI_Income_Poverty_Unemployment_Livability.csv for further analysis.

ML Analaysis

Predicting Air Quality Index (AQI)

We compares two predictive approaches:

  1. Baseline Linear Regression: Predicts tomorrows AQI based on todays value.
  2. Random Forest Classifier: Predicts EPA AQI category (e.g., 'Good', 'Moderate') 13 days ahead.

We evaluate performance using RMSE, R for regression, and accuracy metrics for classification.

1. Baseline: Linear Regression on AQI

This model uses a simple linear regression where the next days AQI is predicted from todays AQI.

  • Technique: Shift AQI column forward by 1.
  • Model: Linear regression (minimize squared error).
  • Evaluation: RMSE and R.

Why It Matters:

This serves as a benchmark. A high RMSE (~28.6) shows AQI is difficult to predict precisely. It highlights why categorizing AQI may be more practical.

2. Random Forest: Predicting EPA Categories

We classify the AQI category (e.g., Good, Moderate, Unhealthy) based on environmental and pollution data.

Features:
  • Rolling averages of AQI and pollutants
  • Seasonal signals via sine/cosine day-of-year encoding
  • Lagged features and future shifted AQI labels
Model:
  • RandomForestClassifier in a pipeline with StandardScaler
  • Models trained for predicting 1, 2, and 3 days ahead
  • Evaluation: Exact accuracy and within-one-category accuracy

  • Random forest classifier reliably predicts AQI categories and is more useful for public health decisions.

This supports a key insight: categorical predictions (e.g., EPA labels) are more actionable and robust than exact AQI predictions.

Results

  • High temperature and ozone levels together lead to the worst AQI (up to 106.3).
  • Summer has the highest median AQI (71), indicating seasonal pollution spikes.
  • Counties with high poverty and unemployment experience worse AQI (74.7) than more affluent areas (54.9).
  • Income-AQI trends show slight improvement in the highest income group.
  • ML Prediction: Our linear regression model projects the average AQI in 2024 to increase further, supporting the observed upward trend in pollution levels.

Overall

Our results show that environmental and socioeconomic stressors drive higher AQI, with disadvantaged communities most affected. The ML model confirms that AQI is likely to worsen without intervention, highlighting the need for targeted, equitable environmental policies.
